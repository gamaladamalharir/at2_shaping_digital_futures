Shaping Digital Futures

AT2 Design Solution

A sustainable training development syllabus addressing educational inequity in rural Indonesia through ICT-integrated pedagogy, community-driven design, and low-tech accessible experiences.

🌍 Context: Rural Indonesia
Wicked Problem

Context & Problem

The use of ICT in the Indonesian education system is not implemented equally. In rural areas, educational and bureaucratic stakeholders often exist in an inharmonious relationship. Resistance to ICT, inadequate training, and a lack of internet infrastructure and facilities from the government all worsen the situation. Hermawan et al. (2018) found that the use of ICT in education and management in Indonesia is still low.

Indonesia's geographical diversity means different regions have vastly different levels of ICT literacy. Despite government policies through the Ministry of Education and Culture's Strategic Plan (2010–2014) making ICT integration a priority, a wicked cycle persists.

Government policy does not cover all rural schools, neglecting infrastructure — which causes low teacher capability in ICT integration. Even where facilities exist, not all teachers are fully technology literate. This resistance to technology adoption due to unfamiliarity and lack of training (UNICEF, 2021) eventually caused low ICT literacy (Mirfani, 2019). The unequal infrastructure investment created a human capacity gap that ultimately restricted students' access to digital tools and online learning resources.

The Wicked Cycle

Unequal Infrastructure → Capacity Gap → Student Disengagement → Low Outcomes → Policy Pressure ↺

Design Thinking

Model Overview & Rationale

A hybrid approach combining the Stanford d.school five-stage model at the macro level and ADDIE at the micro level to ensure empathy, cultural relevance, and pedagogical rigor.

Macro Level
Stanford d.school
Empathise→ Define→ Ideate→ Prototype→ Test

Guides the overall project from empathy stages considering wicked problems across multiple stakeholders, through define and ideate which anticipate cultural resistance and capacity gaps, into prototype and test stages operated via ADDIE. As Tu et al. (2018) suggests, this model is a set of creativity theories for solving both practical and social problems.

Micro Level
ADDIE Model
Analyse→ Design→ Develop→ Implement→ Evaluate

Specifically operated in the prototype and test stages as a system approach to training (SAT). Spector et al. (2014) suggests ADDIE equips evaluation and revision checks at every stage. Shakeel et al. (2023) confirms this model helps instructional designers comprehensively evaluate different stages, while Caws (2013) and Colpaert (2006) note it facilitates technology integration into pedagogy.

Design Aim

To develop a sustainable training development syllabus that focuses on ICT integrated pedagogy to address educational inequity in rural Indonesia by supporting low-tech, accessible, and community-driven teaching and learning experiences — positioning technology as a collaborative learning support rather than a standalone solution.

Key Insights

The use of ICT/EdTech in teaching is rarely about willingness — rather, the lack of confidence and minimum training. Students, particularly in areas with limited device access, showed a pattern of disengagement, many having never used a digital learning tool in class. School leaders experience a structural dilemma: expected to drive innovation while operating within limited resources and complex bureaucracy. Government stakeholders acknowledged oversight limitations in remote areas. MGMP/teacher communities hold the biggest chance to pivot the situation through peer-to-peer knowledge transfer, but this lacks structure and sustainability.

Artefact 2.1 — Problem Cluster Diagram

Cluster 1
Digital Confidence Gap

Teachers avoid ICT not because of unwillingness but from lack of confidence and training.

Design training must be grounded and familiar at first, building confidence before introducing progressive methods.
Cluster 2
Access Gap

Unequal device and internet access excludes students from digital learning.

Prioritise offline-first and low-tech tools so learning does not depend on connectivity.
Cluster 3
Sustainability Gap

Peer-to-peer knowledge transfer exists informally within MGMP but lacks structure.

Embed sustainability mechanisms into the training syllabus so it outlasts any individual facilitator.
Artefact 2.2 — Persona: Budi (hypothetical)

A rural teacher in South Sumatra who relies on familiar conventional methods to maintain confidence and avoid embarrassment. Learns best through hands-on practice and grounded methods rather than formal instruction. Becomes irrelevant from training when tools feel too advanced for his low-connectivity classroom.

🎯 Design Implication: The training syllabus must start with grounded, peer-led activities that build confidence gradually, using offline-first tools that adapt to the realities and cultural aspects of rural South Sumatra classrooms.

Selected Concept: Peer-led Syllabus

Selected as most practical because it directly addresses all three design tensions within a single integrated syllabus, can be delivered entirely by MGMP facilitators without dependency on external trainers or stable internet, and absorbs offline-first tools and Kahoot! as a starter within its modular structure. As Auernhammer & Roth (2021) argue, great innovation management supports a safe environment to try, fail, and grow — exactly what the peer-led syllabus offers.

ADDIE – Implement

Sessions run within existing fortnightly MGMP meetings — no new infrastructure needed. Attendance is voluntary but encouraged through peer recognition and practical outcomes.

Stage 5 — ADDIE + Kirkpatrick

Test & Evaluate

The ADDIE evaluation stage enhanced with Kirkpatrick's 4-Level Evaluation Model to provide a comprehensive, systematic assessment of the intervention.

1
Level 1
Reaction

Did teachers find the sessions relevant, engaging, and well-facilitated?

Post-workshop satisfaction survey (5-min, Likert scale)

Target: 70% rate safety and relevance as 4 or above

2
Level 2
Learning

Did teacher knowledge and confidence shift after the 3 sessions?

Pre/post survey — 10 identical confidence questions

Target: Measurable shift in at least 60% of participants

3
Level 3
Behaviour

Did teachers actually apply gamification in their classrooms after the workshop?

Observation checklist + teacher self-report (2–4 weeks)

Target: At least one gamified activity used in class

4
Level 4
Results

Did the intervention produce meaningful change at the school and student level?

Student feedback form + school leader interview + policy brief

Target: Positive student feedback; MGMP guide ready for independent use

Co-Design

Stakeholder Reflection

Every stakeholder in this project is a co-designer rather than a passive participant, each contributing through their unique gap and tension.

Stakeholder + InputDesign Decision
Rural Teacher: Lack of confidence and fear of tryingRemoves all performance pressure from first contact with gamification
School Principal: Concern about limited resources and school facilitiesEmbedded syllabus within contextual MGMP trainers and teachers needs
MGMP Coordinator: Teachers trust peer modelling more than external expert trainingFrom instructor to guide; Sessions 2 and 3 are entirely teacher-led
Student Representative: Current lessons feel repetitive and passiveAdded student engagement feedback form in Test phase to ensure student voices are heard
District Education Officer: Wants measurable outcomes for reportingAdded Kirkpatrick Level 4 evaluation and a one-page summary output for policy reporting

Tension 1 — Accountability vs Capacity

Teachers are evaluated on ICT use but not trained for it. Resolution: The workshop addresses capability before compliance.

Tension 2 — Principal Compliance vs Teacher Agency

Principals want visible results; teachers want safe spaces. Resolution: Voluntary participation with peer recognition, not top-down mandate.

Section 4

Critical Review of the Design

✅
Strengths

Cultural alignment: The peer-led structure mirrors Indonesian values of gotong royong and musyawarah, overcoming the digital confidence and sustainable gap.

Scalable by design: Trained teachers are encouraged to share learning within the MGMP cluster, allowing the intervention to continue beyond the initial cycle (Voogt et al., 2015).

TPACK integration: Even in low-tech facilities, the workshop integrates technological, pedagogical, and content knowledge without abandoning any element.

Comprehensive evaluation: The Kirkpatrick four-level framework provides a measurable, multi-dimensional assessment of the design.

⚠️
Limitations & Challenges

Surface-level risk: No guarantee that Kahoot! or game-based activities produce deep pedagogical shift rather than surface-level student-centred teaching.

Internet dependency: Kahoot! still requires internet connectivity, creating a dependency even within a "low-tech contingency" design.

Long-term sustainability: Without a clear succession plan, the MGMP coordinator's capacity to sustain the program beyond Month 3 remains uncertain.

This project's main problem circulated around teachers' use of digital tools. After engaging with stakeholders, it became clear the real barrier was confidence, not skill. That changed everything about how the sessions were designed. The wicked cycle persists not because of the problems themselves, but because of the neglect of the connectedness between stakeholders — creating an everlasting tension that reinforces itself.
